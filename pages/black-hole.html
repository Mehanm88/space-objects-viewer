Black Hole

A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing — not even light or other electromagnetic waves — can escape once past the event horizon. The theory of general relativity predicts that a sufficiently compact mass can deform spacetime to form a black hole.

Types of Black Holes

  • Stellar black holes — formed when massive stars collapse at the end of their life cycle
  • Supermassive black holes — found at the center of most galaxies, millions to billions of solar masses
  • Intermediate black holes — mid-range mass, still being studied
  • Primordial black holes — hypothetical, formed soon after the Big Bang

Key Facts

  • The nearest known black hole is about 1,560 light-years from Earth
  • The first image of a black hole was captured in 2019 (M87*)
  • Sagittarius A* is the supermassive black hole at the center of our Milky Way
  • Time slows down near a black hole due to gravitational time dilation
